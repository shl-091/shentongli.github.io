Projects

Project 1: Exploration of Recipes on Food.com | Univariate/Bivariate Analysis, Hypothesis Testing, Data Training

- Cooperated with a partner, scraping data from https://www.food.com/ to get the user's overall feedback of recipes on the website.
- By building a model, we predicted the calories of a recipe due to people's pursue of healthier diet.
- Used Univariate Analysis, Bivariate Analysis, Assessment of Missingness, and Hypothesis Testing to analyze user's rating of recipes. The detailed information can be found here: https://xgui17.github.io/Recipe-Analysis/
- Used Linear-Regression type of model, K-fold Cross-validation, and Fairness Analysis to construct the model which can predict the calorie. The detailed information can be found here: https://xgui17.github.io/Predicting-Calories/

Project 2: Visualizing Airbnb Market Insights in London | HTML/CSS/JavaScript, Data Interactive Visualizatoin

- Developed website with interactive visualizations to analyze and understand the Airbnb market in London, using a comprehensive Airbnb dataset.
- Designed a network visualization illustrating the advantages and disadvantages of different accommodation areas.
- Created a guest satisfaction map, visually representing average satisfaction scores across different regions in London.

Project 3: Pfizer Vaccine Effectiveness | Permutation Testing, Choropleth, Assessment of Missingness

- Gathered data and used Choropleth to calculate what is the average number of doses received in each country.
- Focused on data of Israeli vaccinated using the Pfizer vaccine to discuss whether the Pfizer vaccine is no longer effective against serious illness, or if getting the vaccine will increase the risk of sickness.
- Solving MCAR and using permutation testing, I got the conclusion that the effectiveness of the vaccine within each age group is higher than the overall effectiveness. In fact, the effectiveness of every age group is above 80%, and for most age groups it is above 90%.
- Using Plotly, learned that the reason for Simpson's paradox mentioned above comes from the fact that, for each dosage level, the sickness rate of older people is greater than the sickness rate for younger people.
